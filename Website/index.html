What the dashboard found

It doesn't just show data.
It points to the problem.

Anyone can build a chart. This dashboard surfaces the story hidden inside five years of data. The exact machine, the exact shift, the exact week things went wrong.

01 · The hidden culprit
It pinpointed two machines quietly driving most of the defects.
Buried in 3,000 production runs, two machines on one shift were failing far more than the rest. The dashboard found them. Imagine what it could find in yours.
Detail: Machines 15 & 18 · Swing shift · 6.58% peak
02 · The turning point
It caught the exact year quality slipped, and proved it was fixable.
One year hit the target. The next reversed it. The dashboard shows precisely when and by how much, turning a vague worry into a clear action.
Detail: 2024 best at 3.44% · 2025 worst at 3.72%
03 · The 80/20 rule
It showed that fixing two defect types would move the needle most.
Out of dozens of defect types, just two dominate year after year. Fix those first and get the biggest quality gain for the least effort.
Detail: Scratch & Mislabel · top 2 in 4 of 5 years
04 · The costly pattern
It revealed machines were being fixed late, not maintained early.
Downtime kept climbing while quality kept falling. The signature of reactive repairs. The fix: maintain before breakdown, not after.
Detail: Downtime +8.3% · 2022 to 2025
05 · The anomaly
It flagged a sudden spike most reports would have missed.
For two months, one product line nearly tripled its defect rate, then recovered. The dashboard caught the blip, the kind of signal that saves money when spotted early.
Detail: Furniture · ~10% peak · Aug–Sep 2023
06 · The machine's eye
It used machine learning to spot what humans overlook.
An algorithm reviewed every run and flagged 60 that didn't fit the pattern, kept on record for engineers to investigate, never quietly deleted.
Detail: Isolation Forest · 60 runs flagged

Machine learning

Not ML for the sake of it.
ML because the data demanded it.

Two models, each chosen for one reason: they work where the obvious approaches fail. Every choice is explained, never assumed.

Catching the unusual
Isolation Forest
The simpler anomaly methods need data points to cluster neatly. This data doesn't. Isolation Forest works precisely because it doesn't rely on that. It isolates the odd ones out, no matter how scattered the data is.
Result: 60 unusual runs flagged for engineers to review
Grouping the machines
K-Means Clustering
Instead of guessing which machines need attention, the data decides. Every machine is sorted into Critical, Monitor, or Good based on its real track record. Language an operations team already understands.
Result: 2 Critical · 8 Monitor · 10 Good machines
Why this dashboard

Every choice built to serve
the person asking the question.

A manager shouldn't need to understand the pipeline to trust the number. These are the questions a quality lead, data architect, or plant manager would ask, each answered by design.

How does the dashboard stay accurate when new data arrives?
It only updates what actually changed. Untouched data is skipped automatically, so the dashboard is always current and never wastes a second reprocessing what it already knows. Always fresh, never stale.
Pipeline
Why do the control limits stay fixed when I change the year?
Because a fair benchmark can't move every time you change the view. The limits are locked to the full five-year baseline, so every year is judged against the same standard. One honest yardstick for all.
Process control
Why do machine ratings stay the same across years?
A machine that struggled for four years shouldn't look healthy because of one good one. Ratings reflect the full history, so a real problem can't hide behind a lucky quarter. The truth, not the highlight reel.
Reliability
What happens to the unusual data points?
They're kept, not deleted. Standard views stay clean for managers, while every flagged anomaly remains on record for engineers who need to dig in. Clean for some, complete for all.
Trust
Why does the chart show one metric at a time?
Because four lines on one chart is noise, not insight. A single selector lets the analyst ask one clear question and get one clear answer. Clarity over clutter.
Design
How was the data cleaned without losing the real story?
Only the genuinely impossible values were removed, a tiny fraction. Everything else was recovered or carefully filled using patterns already in the data, so nothing real was thrown away. Repair before delete.
